Day 10 inverted Round 1. Boston, Edmonton, and Minnesota — three trailing teams whose seasons looked nearly over — won their Game 5s. Two of them (Boston and Edmonton) saved their seasons. The third (Minnesota) went one further: they took the series lead from a Tier 1 team in Tier 1 fashion, controlling pace and winning the moments at Dallas's own arena. By midnight, every series in Round 1 was either decided, tied, or with the previous favourite trailing 3–2. What looked like a closeout cluster turned into the most disruptive single day of the round.

§ 02

Three Game 5s, three reversals.

The trailing team won every single Game 5 on the board today. Boston on a Pastrnak breakaway. Edmonton on a complete first period. Minnesota on outright control. Same pattern, three different reasons.

Series 06 · East  ·  Survival

Boston at Buffalo

Series Standing
Sabres 3 — Bruins 2
  • Boston 2, Buffalo 1 in OT. Pastrnak's breakaway 9:14 into overtime — caught Buffalo on a line change, Lindholm's stretch pass found him in stride, fake across, slip inside the right post. Boston staves off elimination, series back to Boston for Game 6 Friday.
  • Buffalo got their first power-play goal in nearly a month — Dahlin one-timer in the right circle in the first period. The 0-for-17 series-long drought ended. Didn't matter.
  • Elias Lindholm tied it in the second on a bad-angle bouncer through traffic — exactly the kind of net-front goal Boston's structure had been failing to generate all series. The shape of the game had changed.
  • Swayman finally got his swing performance: 25 saves, including the critical Zucker stop 3:30 into OT. After being chased in Game 4, this was the goalie Boston needed all series.
  • Buffalo's Lyon was good (27 saves). Not the difference. Boston was simply more dangerous than they'd been in any prior game. They played without Arvidsson again, and still found their best 60 minutes.
  • The series math: Buffalo still leads 3–2 with home ice in Game 7 if needed. But every game from here is a knife fight. Boston has finally shown the structure they need; Buffalo's forecheck didn't dominate the way it had in Games 2–4.
Catching Buffalo on a line change, Hampus Lindholm fed a pass up the right boards to find Pastrnak in stride just as he crossed the blue line. — Associated Press
Boston's Path
Repeat Game 5. Swayman has to be the difference, the breakouts have to stay clean, McAvoy and Lindholm have to play like top-pair defensemen. Game 6 at home Friday — they've finally got the formula. Now they need to do it twice more.
Buffalo's Path
The forecheck pressure that defined Games 2–4 needs to come back. Lyon was good but didn't steal a game — and Boston is going to be harder to forecheck in the TD Garden environment. Game 6 in Boston is now genuinely closer to even than 3–2 implies.
Series Outlook · Updated
Sabres in 7. The structural advantages haven't disappeared — Buffalo is still faster, deeper, and forechecking better than Boston for stretches. But the Bruins have one win in their pocket and a Game 7 in Buffalo would now feel different than it would have 24 hours ago. The closeout that looked inevitable on Sunday is now far from guaranteed.
Series 02 · West  ·  Flipped

Minnesota at Dallas

Series Standing
Wild 3 — Stars 2
  • Minnesota 4, Dallas 2 in Dallas. The Wild now lead 3–2 and head home for Game 6 with a chance to advance for the first time since 2015. They've lost their last nine playoff series, including to Dallas in 2016 and 2023. This time looks different.
  • Boldy's tiebreaking power-play goal with 32 seconds left in the second period — one of the most damning end-of-period goals you'll see in playoff hockey. Boldy also had a goal called back earlier (interference). It's his fourth playoff goal, plus the Game 4 OT winner.
  • Kaprizov is now dictating the series. 1 goal, 2 assists, including the empty-netter to seal it. He's not just producing — he's creating the dangerous sequences. The most impactful player in this series wears Wild green, not Stars green.
  • Mats Zuccarello returned after a three-game absence and scored 3:51 in. Minnesota's depth got a real piece back, and the production was immediate.
  • Wallstedt had 20 saves — 11 of them in the third period when Dallas pushed hardest. He's been the calm Minnesota needed, and the goalie Dallas hasn't had an answer for.
  • Dallas got Robertson again (5 straight games with a goal), Heiskanen on the power play. The pieces are producing. The team isn't dictating. That's been the series story, and Game 5 confirmed it on their home ice.
Minnesota is not just competing with a Tier 1 team. They are dictating the series — and they did it on Dallas's ice.
Minnesota's Path
Game 6 at home Thursday with a chance to advance. Don't change a thing. Wallstedt holds the line, Kaprizov drives play, Boldy finishes, the structure neutralizes Dallas's offensive layers. This is the formula. Run it back.
Dallas's Path
This is now an identity crisis. Dallas's preferred style of game just got beaten on Dallas's ice. They need to open it up — but doing so hands Minnesota's goaltending more chances. They need a swing performance from Oettinger they haven't gotten. They need somebody beyond Robertson to finish at 5v5. None of those are sure things.
Series Outlook · Updated
Wild in 6, real chance. Minnesota is now a Tier 1 disruptor, not a Tier 2 team punching up. They've out-executed Dallas at Dallas's own style of hockey — that's the championship trait that wins playoff series. Dallas faces elimination at the Xcel Energy Center on Thursday, with Wallstedt waiting and a building that hasn't had a Round 2 series in over a decade. This is now Minnesota's series to lose.
Series 08 · West  ·  Survival

Anaheim at Edmonton

Series Standing
Ducks 3 — Oilers 2
  • Edmonton 4, Anaheim 1. The Oilers finally played a complete playoff game — scored on the first shot 2:22 in, built a 3–0 first-period lead, chased Lukas Dostal after three goals on nine shots. Series back to Anaheim for Game 6 Thursday.
  • The story is what Edmonton finally didn't do: blow the lead. Through four games they had given up six separate leads. Game 5: scored first, never trailed, didn't collapse. That's the first time they've played 60 minutes the right way in this series.
  • Draisaitl took over with two goals — including a wicked PP one-timer that tied Wayne Gretzky for the most postseason power-play goals in Oilers franchise history (23). Bouchard had three assists. McDavid and Nugent-Hopkins had two each. Stars produced when stars were required.
  • Connor Ingram, back in net, made 29 saves. The goaltending finally stabilized. Edmonton has now started Ingram, Jarry, then Ingram again across this series — and the third try produced the goalie they needed all along.
  • The historical context is stunning: Edmonton is now 18-3 all-time when scoring first while facing elimination. McDavid passed Adam Oates for the second-most points in NHL history when trailing in a series (only Gretzky has more, with 80). This team has been here before, and they know how to win it.
  • Anaheim outshot Edmonton 24–8 over the final 40 minutes. The Ducks didn't play poorly. They got buried early, couldn't create the chaos they thrive in, and ran into a goalie who didn't break. Their structural strengths got muted by Edmonton's first complete period of the series.
Desperation level is high. — Leon Draisaitl, post-game
Edmonton's Path
Repeat the formula. Score first. Stay with Ingram. Simplify offense. Don't try to win pretty. Game 6 in Anaheim is the test of whether Game 5 was a desperation game or a sustainable shift. If they play another 60-minute version of this, the series flips again.
Anaheim's Path
Don't get rattled. The Ducks have been the better team for most of this series — Game 5 was the exception, not the rule. Get a stronger start (Dostal got chased; need him to be ready). Force chaos. Lean on the depth that produced 20 goals in the first four games. Game 6 at home is still a heavy favourite for Anaheim.
Series Outlook · Updated
Ducks still in 6, slight lean. Edmonton produced one good game — they need two. The historical numbers favour them when they're desperate, but the series-long pattern still favours Anaheim. Game 6 at the Honda Center with the same chaotic-game formula that won them three games is the closeout scenario the Ducks have to expect.
§ 03

The patterns shifting.

Round 1's tier reads aren't holding up cleanly anymore. Three Game 5s went to the trailing team. The framework is being tested in real time.

Tier Update · Day 10
Minnesota → Tier 1 disruptor. They're no longer playing above their tier — they're controlling a series against a Tier 1 team, on the road, in their preferred style. That's the championship trait. Dallas → Tier 2. The profile is still elite, but they've been out-executed in their own kind of game on their own ice. They need to win two straight to validate Tier 1 status — until then, they don't have it.
TREND / 01

Trailing teams won every Game 5.

  • Boston (down 3–1) → won 2–1 OT
  • Edmonton (down 3–1) → won 4–1
  • Minnesota (tied 2–2) → won 4–2 to take the lead
  • Pittsburgh's Game 5 yesterday: same pattern (down 3–1, won 3–2)
  • Four straight Game 5s won by the trailing team. That's not variance. That's desperation outworking expectation.
TREND / 02

Goaltending swaps keep working.

  • Buffalo: Lyon over Luukkonen → won 3 in a row
  • Pittsburgh: Silovs over Jarry → won 2 of 3 since
  • Edmonton: Ingram → Jarry → Ingram → 4–1 win
  • Boston: stayed with Swayman, finally got the goalie they needed
  • Sometimes the swap is the move. Sometimes the patience is. The trailing teams are figuring it out either way.
TREND / 03

Stars do still matter — when they show up.

  • Pastrnak: OT breakaway, season-saving goal
  • Draisaitl: 2G + tied Gretzky franchise record on the PP
  • Kaprizov: 1G+2A, dictating sequences
  • McDavid: 63 career playoff points trailing in a series — 2nd all-time behind only Gretzky
  • For ten days we said system beats stars. Day 10 said: stars and system together is what wins Game 5s. Both are needed.

The Closeout Math

Five active series, four with the favourite leading 3–2 or trailing 3–2. By Friday night, the Round 2 bracket could be 6 of 8 decided. If favourites close (BUF, ANA, PHI, TBL or VGK), Round 2 starts mid-week. If they don't, this becomes the most upset-heavy first round in years.

The Take
Day 8 delivered the verdicts. Day 9 cast doubt. Day 10 inverted them. Three trailing teams won. Two saved their seasons. One took control. The bracket that looked nearly settled by Sunday is now actively unsettling itself. The favourites who close from here will have earned every inch — because the trailing teams have learned that the series isn't over until someone takes that fourth win, and right now, nobody's taking them quietly.
