Day 3 is when the adjustments start showing up. Tampa inserted a heavier lineup, matched Montreal hit-for-hit, and let Kucherov do the deciding — a 3–2 overtime win that ties their series 1–1. Boston came to Buffalo with a different kind of adjustment: punish the goaltending. Three second-period goals, a 16-second knockout to open the third, and the series flipped from "Buffalo in control" to "series reset, Boston on the rise." The upsets are real. Holding them is a different skill entirely.

§ 02

Two Game 2s, two reversals.

Tampa adjusted their physicality. Boston adjusted their patience. Both got paid.

Series 05 · East  ·  Reversed

Montreal at Tampa Bay

Series Standing
Canadiens 1 — Lightning 1
  • Tampa 3, Montreal 2 — in overtime. The adjustment worked. Tampa dressed a heavier lineup, matched Montreal's physicality from puck drop, and waited for one mistake.
  • Thirty penalty minutes in the first period alone. Scrums, fights (Slafkovsky dropped the gloves), after-whistle chaos. This wasn't a skill game — it was a war.
  • Montreal actually took control. 2–1 lead late in the third, Josh Anderson wrecking people, Hutson scoring on the power play.
  • Then the turn. Slafkovsky turnover on a clearing attempt. Kucherov made him pay. Tied game — 40 minutes of Montreal work erased in one shift.
  • The missed chance: late Montreal power play after an Anderson hit-from-behind. Zero goals. In playoffs, that's the window you don't get back.
  • OT: Moser walks a puck and beats Dobes clean. Tampa made the play. Montreal didn't. That's the whole playoff margin right there.
Montreal controlled stretches. Tampa capitalized on the one moment that mattered. That's why the series is tied.
Montreal's Path
Clean up the puck management — especially D-zone exits. You can't give elite stars second chances. Finish the power play when it comes late. Keep the physical edge, lose the volatility — the chaos is hurting them as much as Tampa.
Tampa's Path
Keep the heavier lineup. The physical matchup is no longer lopsided. Continue letting Montreal take chances and feed Kucherov the leftovers — the series is tied precisely because Tampa stopped trying to outskate and started trying to outlast.
Series Outlook · Updated
After Game 1 we said Tampa was a clear favourite and the series was open. After Game 2, it's a true toss-up. Montreal can dictate play — but until they stop handing Kucherov free goals, they can't win four of these. This is a long series now.
Series 06 · East  ·  Reversed

Boston at Buffalo

Series Standing
Sabres 1 — Bruins 1
  • Boston 4, Buffalo 2. The Day 1 thesis just got rewritten. The Bruins we said "can't play anything but goalie-dependent hockey" came in and scored four.
  • Buffalo started strong. Generated pressure, controlled chances early. Then the second period happened.
  • Three Boston goals in the second — an Arvidsson 5-hole beat, a soft dump-in goal, a Zacha power-play redirect. This wasn't dominance. It was exploitation.
  • Arvidsson again, 16 seconds into the third. Luukkonen pulled immediately. Game effectively over before most of the crowd sat down.
  • Buffalo pushed late (Byram, Krebs) to make it 4–2, but by then it was scoreboard management, not a real comeback window
  • Sabres power play now 0-for-9 in the series. 0-for-31 dating back. This is a structural problem, not variance.
  • Faceoffs: 18–2 Boston in the first period. That's how you dictate territory without dominating possession.
Buffalo played well early. Boston capitalized on a short window of weakness. That's all it took to flip the game and the series momentum.
Buffalo's Path
Make the goaltending decision. Luukkonen's rhythm is gone; the soft goals aren't going to stop themselves. Fix the power play or stop taking them — 0-for-9 is actively losing. Emotional discipline: Thompson off the ice during the comeback push is a self-inflicted wound.
Boston's Path
Keep pressuring the goaltending — every shot matters against a netminder this shaky. Win the faceoff battle again and the possession follows. The depth finally showed up (Arvidsson, Zacha); keep leaning on it instead of waiting for top-line magic.
Series Outlook · Updated
After Game 1 we said Buffalo was firmly in control. After Game 2, the series is reset — and momentum is with Boston. The question isn't whether Buffalo can still win it; it's whether they can stabilize goaltending and special teams before this becomes a trend.
§ 03

The pattern emerging.

Three Day 1 reads, three Game 2 reversals. That's not coincidence — that's a tournament-wide signal.

TREND / 01

Day 1 reads don't survive Game 2.

  • Minnesota stole Game 1. Dallas answered 4–2 the next night.
  • Montreal stole Game 1. Tampa answered 3–2 in OT.
  • Buffalo took Game 1 at home. Boston flipped it 4–2 on Buffalo's ice.
  • Three for three. The Game 1 storyline almost always gets rewritten in Game 2.
TREND / 02

Special teams are the sharpest edge.

  • Buffalo's PP: 0-for-9 this series. 0-for-31 dating back. They're down 1–1 because of it.
  • Pittsburgh: 0-for-7 in the playoffs, shorthanded goal against. Down 0–2.
  • Dallas went 1-for-1 to beat Minnesota. Montreal's PP won Game 1 against Tampa, then went silent in Game 2.
  • Every series is being decided here first.
TREND / 03

Structure beats stars — except when it doesn't.

  • Edmonton won without a McDavid point. Boston won with secondary scoring (Arvidsson, Zacha).
  • Kucherov alone decided Game 2 for Tampa
  • The real rule: structure wins most minutes. Stars win the last one.
§ 04

What to watch next.

Three Game 2s coming in 48 hours. Every one has an upset watch attached.

✓
Slafkovsky's Response

Hat trick in Game 1, game-losing turnover in Game 2. Young players often follow exactly that arc. Is Game 3 another superstar night, or does the mistake get into his head?

✓
Sabres Goaltending

Luukkonen got pulled. The backup is unproven. Buffalo needs to make a call — and whichever goalie gets Game 3 will need to answer a question the team hasn't had to ask in months.

✓
Utah's 60 Minutes

Led twice in Game 1 — for 40 minutes. If they can't survive a full 60 of Vegas pressure, Day 1's "Close" becomes Day 4's "Sweep."

✓
LA's Finishing

LA had ~57% xG at 5v5 in Game 1 and lost. Game 2 tells us if that was a variance event or a systemic problem. Missing tap-ins twice in a row is a pattern.

The Take
Day 1 told us what might be true. Day 2 tested it. Day 3 is showing us the real shape of the tournament. Three Day 1 reads, three Game 2 reversals. The favourites are adjusting faster than the storylines can settle.
