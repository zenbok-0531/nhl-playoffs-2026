§ 02

Four verdicts in one day.

Buffalo's stranglehold. Colorado's sweep. Tampa's flip. Anaheim's OT closer. Four different stories — but all of them point at the same Round 1 conclusion: structure beats stars, and adjustment beats panic.

Series 06 · East  ·  Stranglehold

Buffalo at Boston

Series Standing
Sabres 3 — Bruins 1
  • Buffalo 6, Boston 1. The numbers tell the story: 4–0 after one period. 19–5 in shots. Ten Boston giveaways in the first 20 minutes. By the end of one, this game was over.
  • Three of Buffalo's four first-period goals came directly off forced turnovers during Boston breakouts. This isn't variance — it's a structural failure that the Bruins' top defensive pair (McAvoy and Lindholm) couldn't solve.
  • The Sabres' youngest line — Doan (24), Östlund (22), Benson (20) — drove the game from the drop of the puck. They didn't play together in the regular season. None had played a playoff game before this series. They've found chemistry through style, not familiarity.
  • Twelve Sabres recorded a point. Four had multi-point games. That's a depth signal that's hard to fake — the kind of stat that says a team is deeper than its star chart suggests.
  • Bowen Byram is now the Sabres' most valuable player through four games. Three goals in the postseason. On ice for four of six in this game. The kind of defenseman performance that shifts series outcomes.
  • Two more pieces of bad news for Boston: Arvidsson left after just 3:13 of ice time with an upper-body injury (he was their second-best forward in Game 2's lone win), and the Sabres' power play finally scored after 35 dry chances dating back to the regular season.
Buffalo's speed and tenacity on the forecheck have started to wear down a Boston team that struggles breaking the puck out to begin with. — Fluto Shinzawa & Matthew Fairburn, The Athletic
Boston's Path
Not small tweaks — structural adjustments. Shorter breakout routes. More chip-and-retrieve, less stretch passing. McAvoy and Lindholm have to fight the puck less, support each other more. Possible defensive pairing reset. None of this happens cleanly in 48 hours.
Buffalo's Path
Don't get cute. Forecheck. Forecheck. Forecheck. Stay with Lyon in net. Keep rolling four lines. Keep Byram on the ice. The young line is the secret weapon — let them eat the matchups Boston tries to use against the top six.
Series Outlook · Updated
Sabres in 5. The Bruins' problems aren't about effort or coaching — they're about whether their structure can hold up against speed, and through four games, it can't. Boston's top defensive pair needs to solve Buffalo's forecheck inside 48 hours. That's not a realistic ask.
Series 04 · West  ·  Sweep · Advanced

Colorado at LA Kings

Series Final
Avalanche 4 — Kings 0
  • Colorado swept the LA Kings in four games to become the second team into Round 2, joining Carolina. Two sweeps in 48 hours, both clinched on the road, both by teams whose stars barely showed up.
  • The system story is the headline. MacKinnon and Makar — the Avalanche's two best players — produced minimal offense across the four games. Colorado advanced anyway. That's the same template Carolina just used: depth + structure + goaltending = advancement.
  • Lehkonen, Necas, and the third line drove this series the same way Stankoven's line drove Carolina's. The Avs' bottom-six produced more than their stars. When a team can do that for four straight games, the opponent runs out of answers.
  • LA never had the right matchup. Kopitar, Doughty, and the Kings' veteran core got out-skated and out-systemed. They were hoping age and experience would slow Colorado down. It didn't. The Avs' speed was too much, and their structure was airtight.
  • Goaltending was steady — not spectacular — but didn't have to be. Colorado's defensive system kept high-danger chances scarce, the same way Carolina's did against Ottawa.
  • The implication for Round 2: if MacKinnon and Makar wake up, this team becomes terrifying. They're already a contender on system alone. Adding their stars back into a working machine is the upgrade no one wants to face.
Colorado advanced without their stars producing. That should terrify the rest of the bracket.
What This Was
A clean four-game sweep that exposed LA's identity as too veteran, too slow, and too structured to survive Colorado's speed. Not blowouts. Not statement games. Just four sixty-minute clinics in playing the right way every shift.
What This Says
Colorado is the most dangerous team in the bracket right now — and that's true even without MacKinnon and Makar producing. Their depth scoring is real. Their goaltending is dependable. Their structure has been good enough to advance with their stars dormant. If those stars rejoin the equation in Round 2, the equation breaks the other way.
Round 2 Awaits
Colorado now waits for the winner of Dallas–Minnesota (currently 2–2). Either matchup is favourable for the Avs. Dallas would be the bigger structural challenge; Minnesota the more chaotic one. Either way, Colorado has 7+ days of rest before Round 2 starts. That's the kind of reset that matters in May.
Round 1 Verdict
Two teams have advanced. Both swept. Both did it without their stars. Carolina and Colorado have given the bracket the same lesson: the playoffs reward the team with the right system, not the team with the better stars. The favourites still standing in Round 2 are going to be the ones who learned this lesson before April.
Series 05 · East  ·  Flipped

Tampa at Montreal

Series Standing
Tied 2 — 2
  • Tampa 3, Montreal 2. Down 2–0 in the second period and headed for a 3–1 series hole, Tampa scored three unanswered to take Game 4 and home ice back. Series tied 2–2, with Game 5 in Tampa on Wednesday.
  • The turning point: Max Crozier — playing his first game of the series — destroyed Juraj Slafkovsky with a clean hit at center ice with 2:12 left in the second. No penalty called. Slafkovsky labored to the locker room. The Lightning bench erupted. Tampa scored on the next 4-on-4 to make it 2–1.
  • Brandon Hagel, with two third-period goals, is now six goals deep in the series. Cooper reunited Hagel with Kucherov and Point — the obvious move that finally paid off. Kucherov's shot off Hagel's face with 4:53 left was the winner.
  • The Athletic-confirmed structural problem for Montreal: their top line of Suzuki–Caufield–Slafkovsky still has zero 5v5 goals through four games. Caufield (51 regular-season goals) finally scored — on the power play. The 5v5 drought continues.
  • Montreal's coaching response was honest. St. Louis on the momentum swing: "I don't think we managed it good enough... that's a veteran team. They're very good at drawing penalties." The lesson Montreal hasn't learned yet — how to slow a game down after losing control.
  • The series shape just changed completely. Tampa has home ice back. Cooper gets full last-change advantage in Games 5 and 7. The Cirelli–Gourde shutdown line just got more dangerous against Suzuki's line. Without 5v5 production from the kids, Montreal's path narrows fast.
The Crozier hit changed everything. — Arpon Basu & Pierre LeBrun, The Athletic
Tampa's Path
Stay structured. Stay physical. Cooper's adjustments worked — Hagel-Point-Kucherov is suddenly the most dangerous line in the series, Cirelli-Gourde is taking Suzuki's line off the board. Vasilevskiy needs only modest performance with that defensive structure in front of him. Cooper now has the matchups he wants.
Montreal's Path
Get Caufield going at 5v5. Get somebody on the top line going at 5v5. The Hutson–OT–power-play formula is brittle, and Tampa just demonstrated they can break it. Find a way to weather a momentum swing without taking penalties. Hard ask for a young team that's never played a Game 5 in a tied series.
Series Outlook · Updated
Coin-flip series, slight lean Tampa. The veterans just took a punch, returned a harder one, and walked out of Montreal with home ice. Game 5 in Tampa is now the pivot point of the entire series — winner controls the rest of it. Habs need their top line awake. If they don't get there in Game 5, this becomes Tampa's series.
Series 08 · West  ·  Stranglehold

Anaheim at Edmonton

Series Standing
Ducks 3 — Oilers 1
  • Anaheim 4, Edmonton 3 in OT. Ryan Poehling's controversial goal bounced in off an Edmonton skate 2:29 into overtime. The Ducks now lead 3–1 — three straight wins after losing Game 1. Edmonton's season is on Game 5 in Edmonton on Tuesday.
  • The bigger pattern: Edmonton led 2–0 early and 3–2 in the third — and lost in OT anyway. Same story as Game 3. Edmonton can build momentum. They cannot protect it. That's the entire series in one sentence.
  • Anaheim has now scored 20 goals in four games against the Oilers. They're scoring in every situation: power play, rush, net-front, depth lines. Edmonton's defensive structure improved from Game 3 — and they conceded four anyway. This is no longer a fixable problem.
  • Jackson LaCombe leads the entire NHL in postseason scoring with 8 points — from defense, on a wild-card team. He had two assists tonight. The series is being driven by depth, defense activation, and unexpected names.
  • The McDavid story sharpened. AP confirmed he's "suspected to be dealing with an injury." He had 2 assists and was denied twice on breakaways by Lukas Dostal in the final minutes. He's participating, not dictating. That's the verdict on his Round 1.
  • Edmonton's goaltending changed — Tristan Jarry started, made 34 saves, played his first game since April 8 and was actually the better goalie at times. Didn't matter. The structural problems aren't goaltending alone. Anaheim is just better, in more situations, with more contributors.
Oilers down to last life after losing Game 4 to "unlucky bounce." — Mark Spector, Sportsnet
The Bounce vs The Truth
The OT goal was lucky, off a skate, controversial on review. Edmonton fans will rightfully argue the call. But blaming the bounce misses the deeper issue: Anaheim has now scored 20 goals in 4 games, blown leads in 3 straight, won every momentum war that mattered. You don't lose a series to bounces. You lose them to patterns.
Anaheim's Path
Don't change anything. Power play scoring in 8 straight games. Depth scoring at every line. LaCombe leading the entire postseason in scoring. Stay aggressive on Edmonton's defensemen, lean on Dostal making the McDavid stops, keep finding goals from unexpected names. Game 5 closes this.
Edmonton's Path
Win one game. The most realistic path: get McDavid healthier (or at least better disguised), play a complete 60 minutes for the first time in this series, hope Jarry stands on his head. None of that is impossible. All of it is unlikely against this version of Anaheim. Forcing Game 6 in Anaheim is the only goal — and it's a long one.
Series Outlook · Updated
Ducks in 5, probably. This isn't a series Edmonton lost to bad luck or one bad game. Anaheim has been the better team for three straight games — fast, deep, opportunistic, structurally sound when the lead changes hands. The 3–1 series lead reflects what the actual hockey has shown: a heavy underdog has been playing like the better team. McDavid's playoff career just got another chapter that won't help his legacy.
§ 03

The verdicts landing.

Round 1 is starting to deliver final answers. The patterns we identified in Days 1–3 are now hardening into series-deciding truths.

TREND / 01

Speed beats structure when structure cracks.

  • Buffalo: forecheck overwhelmed Boston's breakout. Up 3–1.
  • Utah: speed broke Vegas's structure. Up 2–1.
  • Anaheim: chaos exposed Edmonton's defensive coverage. Up 2–1.
  • The teams playing fast are beating the teams that needed to be perfect. Three favourites trailing because their structure couldn't survive pressure.
TREND / 02

Depth scoring is the contender signal.

  • Buffalo: 12 players with a point in Game 4. Four multi-point.
  • Carolina: Stankoven's third line drove the sweep. Jarvis pointless.
  • Colorado: 3–0 with MacKinnon and Makar combining for 2 points.
  • Philly: fourth line scored two Game 3 goals.
  • Every Round 1 leader is winning with depth. Every Round 1 trailer is losing without it.
TREND / 03

The young cores have arrived.

  • Buffalo's Doan-Östlund-Benson line: youngest on the team, drove Game 4
  • Anaheim's Sennecke (20), Carlsson (21): scored 42 seconds apart in Game 3
  • Utah's Cooley, Guenther: emerging as the series' difference-makers
  • Montreal's Hutson: OT winner in Game 3, 21 years old
  • Round 1's signature isn't superstars — it's twenty-somethings playing fearlessly.

They beat their opponents with depth, structure, and adjustment. The favourites still standing in Round 2 are going to be the ones who already understood that the playoffs reward systems, not stars.
